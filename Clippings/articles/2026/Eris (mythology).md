In [Greek mythology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology "Greek mythology"), **Eris** ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Ἔρις, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Ancient_Greek "Romanization of Ancient Greek"): *Eris*, [lit.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literal_translation "Literal translation") 'Strife') is the goddess and personification of strife and discord, particularly in war, and in the *Iliad* (where she is the "sister" of [Ares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ares "Ares") the god of war). According to [Hesiod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesiod "Hesiod") she was the daughter of primordial [Nyx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyx "Nyx") (Night), and the mother of a long list of undesirable personified abstractions, such as [Ponos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ponos "Ponos") (Toil), [Limos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limos "Limos") (Famine), Algea (Pains) and [Ate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ate_\(mythology\) "Ate (mythology)") (Delusion). Eris initiated a quarrel between [Hera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hera "Hera"), [Athena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athena "Athena") and [Aphrodite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphrodite "Aphrodite"), which led to the [Judgement of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judgement_of_Paris "Judgement of Paris") and ultimately the [Trojan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_War "Trojan War"). Eris's Roman equivalent is [Discordia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discordia "Discordia"). According to Hesiod, there was another Eris, separate and distinct from Eris the daughter of Nyx, who was beneficial to men.[^2]

## Etymology

The name derives from the noun *eris*, with [stem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_stem "Word stem") *erid-*, which means "strife, discord" and is of uncertain etymology; connections with the verb ὀρίνειν *orínein* "to raise, stir, excite" and the proper name Ἐρινύες *[Erinyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erinyes "Erinyes")* have been suggested. [R. S. P. Beekes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_S._P._Beekes "Robert S. P. Beekes") sees no strong evidence for this relation and excludes the derivation from ἐρείδω *ereídō* "to prop, to support" due to the name's original [ι-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iota "Iota") stem.[^3] Watkins suggested origin from a [Proto-Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European "Proto-Indo-European") root *ere* - meaning "to separate, to adjoin".[^4] The name gave several derivatives in [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek"), including ἐρίζω *erízō* "to fight" and ἔρισμα *érisma* "object of a quarrel".[^3]

## Family

In [Homer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer "Homer") 's *[Iliad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iliad "Iliad")*, Eris is described as the "sister and comrade" of [Ares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ares "Ares"),[^5] though according to [Geoffrey Kirk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Kirk "Geoffrey Kirk") she is "not fully personified" here, and this genealogy is a "purely *ad hoc* description".[^6] Some scholars interpret this passage as indicating she is the daughter of [Zeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeus "Zeus") and [Hera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hera "Hera"), Ares' parents.[^7] However, according to [Hesiod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesiod "Hesiod") 's *[Theogony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theogony "Theogony")*, Eris is the daughter of [Nyx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyx "Nyx") (Night), being among the many children Nyx produced without a partner. These siblings of Eris include personifications—like Eris—of several "loathsome" (*στυγερός* \[stugerós\]) things, such as [Moros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moros "Moros") ("Doom"), [Thanatos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thanatos "Thanatos") ("Death"), the [Moirai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moirai "Moirai") ("Fates"), [Nemesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nemesis "Nemesis") ("Indignation"), [Apate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apate "Apate") ("Deceit"), and [Geras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geras "Geras") ("Old Age").[^8]

Like her mother Nyx, Hesiod has Eris as the mother—with no father mentioned—of many children (the only child of Nyx with offspring) who are also personifications representing various misfortunes and harmful things which, in Eris' case, might be thought to result from discord and strife.[^9] All of Eris' children are little more than allegorizations of the meanings of their names, with virtually no other identity.[^10] The following table lists the children of Eris, as given by Hesiod:[^11]

## Judgement of Paris

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Enrique_Simonet_-_El_Juicio_de_Paris.jpg/250px-Enrique_Simonet_-_El_Juicio_de_Paris.jpg)

El Juicio de Paris by Enrique Simonet, 1904

Eris plays a crucial role in one important myth. She was the initiator of the quarrel between the three Greek goddesses, [Hera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hera "Hera"), [Athena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athena "Athena"), and [Aphrodite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphrodite "Aphrodite"), resolved by the [Judgement of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judgement_of_Paris "Judgement of Paris"), which led to [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_\(mythology\) "Paris (mythology)") ' abduction of [Helen of Troy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helen_of_Troy "Helen of Troy") and the outbreak of the [Trojan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_War "Trojan War").[^70] As the story came to be told, all the gods were invited to the wedding of [Peleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peleus "Peleus") and [Thetis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thetis "Thetis") except Eris. She came anyway, but was refused admission. In anger, she threw a golden apple among the wedding guests inscribed with "For the fairest", which the three goddesses each claimed.[^71]

[Homer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer "Homer") alludes to the Judgement of Paris, but with no mention of Eris.[^72] An account of the story was told in the *[Cypria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cypria "Cypria")*, one of the poems in the [Epic Cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epic_Cycle "Epic Cycle"), which told the entire story of the Trojan War. The *Cypria*, which is the first poem in the Cycle, describes events preceding those that occur in the *Iliad*, the second poem in the Cycle. According to a prose summary of the now lost *Cypria*, Eris, acting according to the plans of Zeus and [Themis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Themis "Themis") to bring about the Trojan War, instigates a *nekios* ('feud') between the three goddesses over "beauty" (presumably over who of the three was the most beautiful), while they were attending the wedding feast of Peleus and Thetis (who would become the parents of [Achilles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achilles "Achilles")). To settle the dispute, Zeus orders the three goddesses to go to [Mount Ida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Ida "Mount Ida") to be judged by Paris. Paris, having been offered Helen by Aphrodite in return for Paris choosing her, does so.[^73]

The fifth-century BC playwright [Euripides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euripides "Euripides") describes the Judgement of Paris several times with no mention of either Eris or an apple.[^74] Later accounts include details, such as the golden [Apple of Discord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_of_Discord "Apple of Discord"), which may or may not have come from the *Cypria*. According to the *[Fabulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fabulae "Fabulae")* of Hyginus, composed somewhere between the first century BC and the late second century AD, all the gods had been invited to the wedding except Eris. Nevertheless, she came to the wedding feast, and when refused entrance, she threw an apple through the doorway, saying that it was for the "fairest", which started the quarrel.[^75] The [satirist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satire "Satire") [Lucian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucian "Lucian") (fl. 2nd century AD) tells us that Eris's apple was "solid gold" and that it was inscribed: "For the queen of Beauty" (ἡ καλὴ λαβέτω).[^76]

## Strife in war

Eris personifies strife, particularly the strife associated with war.[^77] In Homer's *Iliad*, Eris is described as being depicted on both [Athena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athena "Athena") 's battle [aegis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegis "Aegis"), and [Achilles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achilles "Achilles") ' shield, where she appears alongside other war-related personifications: [Phobos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phobos_\(mythology\) "Phobos (mythology)") ("Rout"), [Alke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alke "Alke") ("Valor"), and [Ioke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ioke_\(mythology\) "Ioke (mythology)") ("Assault"), on the aegis, and [Kydoimos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kydoimos "Kydoimos") ("Tumult"), and [Ker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keres "Keres") ("Fate"), on the shield.[^78] Similarly, the [Hesiodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesiodic "Hesiodic") *[Shield of Heracles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shield_of_Heracles "Shield of Heracles")* has Eris depicted on Heracles' shield, also with Phobos, Kydoimos and Ker, as well as other war-related personifications: [Proioxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proioxis "Proioxis") ("Pursuit"), [Palioxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palioxis "Palioxis") ("Rally"), [Homados](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homados "Homados") ("Tumult "), [Phonos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonoi "Phonoi") ("Murder"), and [Androktasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Androktasiai "Androktasiai") ("Slaughter").[^79] Here Eris is described as flying over the head of Phobos ("Fear"):

> In the middle was Fear, made of adamant, unspeakable, glaring backward with eyes shining like fire. His mouth was full of white teeth, terrible, dreadful; and over his grim forehead flew terrible Strife, preparing for the battle-rout of men—cruel one, she took away the mind and sense of any men who waged open war against Zeus’ son \[Heracles\].

—  [Hesiod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesiod "Hesiod"), *[Shield of Heracles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shield_of_Heracles "Shield of Heracles")* [144–150](https://www.loebclassics.com/view/hesiod-shield/2018/pb_LCL503.13.xml); translation by [Glenn W. Most](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glenn_W._Most "Glenn W. Most")

Eris also appears in several battle scenes in the *Iliad*.[^80] However, unlike Apollo, Athena and several other of the Olympians, Eris does not participate in active combat, nor take sides in the war.[^81] Her role in the *Iliad* is that of "the rouser of armies",[^82] urging both armies to fight each other. In Book 4, she is one of the divinities (along with Ares, Athena, [Deimos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deimos_\(deity\) "Deimos (deity)") ("Terror"), and Phobos ("Rout") urging the armies to battle, with head lowered at first, but soon raised up to the heavens:[^83]

> And the Trojans were urged on by Ares, and the Achaeans by flashing-eyed Athene, and Terror, and Rout, and Strife who rages incessantly, sister and comrade of man-slaying Ares; she first rears her crest only a little, but then her head is fixed in the heavens while her feet tread on earth. She it was who now cast evil strife into their midst as she went through the throng, making the groanings of men to increase.

—  [Homer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer "Homer"), *[Iliad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iliad "Iliad")* [4.439–445](https://www.loebclassics.com/view/homer-iliad/1924/pb_LCL170.197.xml); translation by A. T. Murray, revised by William F. Wyatt

She also appears in this "rouser of armies" role in Book 5,[^84] and again in Book 11, where Zeus sends Eris to rouse the Greek army by shouting:[^85]

> Zeus sent Strife to the swift ships of the Achaeans, gruesome Strife, holding in her hands a portent of war. And she stood by Odysseus’ black ship, huge of hull, that was in the middle so that a shout could reach to either end, both to the huts of Aias, son of Telamon, and to those of Achilles; for these had drawn up their shapely ships at the furthermost ends, trusting in their valor and the strength of their hands. There the goddess stood and uttered a great and terrible shout, a shrill cry of war, and in the heart of each man of the Achaeans she roused strength to war and to battle without ceasing. And to them at once war became sweeter than to return in their hollow ships to their dear native land.

—  [Homer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer "Homer"), *[Iliad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iliad "Iliad")* [11.3–14](https://www.loebclassics.com/view/homer-iliad/1924/pb_LCL170.493.xml); translation by A. T. Murray, revised by William F. Wyatt

Her lust for bloodshed is insatiable. Later in Book 11, she is the last of the gods to leave the battlefield, rejoicing as she watches the fighting she has roused.[^86] While in Book 5, she is described as raging unceasingly.[^87]

Hesiod also associates Eris with war. In his *Works and Days*, he says that she "fosters evil war and conflict".[^88] And in his *Theogony*, has the [Hysminai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hysminai "Hysminai") (Battles) and the [Machai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machai "Machai") (Wars) as her children.[^89]

## Another Eris

In addition to the Eris who was the daughter of [Nyx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyx "Nyx") (Night), Hesiod, in his *[Works and Days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Works_and_Days "Works and Days")*, mentions another Eris. He contrasts the two: the former being "blameworthy" who "fosters evil war and conflict", the latter worthy of "praise", have been created by Zeus to foster beneficial competition:[^90]

> So there was not just one birth of Strifes after all, but upon the earth there are two Strifes. One of these a man would praise once he got to know it, but the other is blameworthy; and they have thoroughly opposed spirits. For the one fosters evil war and conflict—cruel one, no mortal loves that one, but it is by necessity that they honor the oppressive Strife, by the plans of the immortals. But the other one gloomy Night bore first; and Cronus’ high-throned son, who dwells in the aether, set it in the roots of the earth, and it is much better for men. It rouses even the helpless man to work. For a man who is not working but who looks at some other man, a rich one who is hastening to plow and plant and set his house in order, he envies him, one neighbor envying his neighbor who is hastening toward wealth: and this Strife is good for mortals.

—  [Hesiod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesiod "Hesiod"), *[Works and Days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Works_and_Days "Works and Days")* [11–24](https://www.loebclassics.com/view/hesiod-works_days/2018/pb_LCL057.87.xml); translation by [Glenn W. Most](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glenn_W._Most "Glenn W. Most")

## Other mentions

[Antoninus Liberalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoninus_Liberalis "Antoninus Liberalis"), in his *Metamorphoses*, involves Eris in the story of [Polytechnus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytechnus "Polytechnus") and [Aëdon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A%C3%ABdon "Aëdon"), who claimed to love each other more than Hera and Zeus. This angered Hera, so she sent Eris to wreak discord upon them.[^91] Eris is mentioned many times in [Quintus Smyrnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quintus_Smyrnaeus "Quintus Smyrnaeus") ' *[Posthomerica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Posthomerica "Posthomerica")*, which covers the period between the end of the *[Iliad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iliad "Iliad")* and the beginning of his *[Odyssey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odyssey "Odyssey")*.[^92] Just as in the *Iliad*, the *Posthomerica* Eris is the instigator of conflict,[^93] does not take sides,[^94] shouts,[^95] and delights in the carnage of battle.[^96] Eris is also mentioned in the *[Dionysiaca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysiaca "Dionysiaca")* of [Nonnus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonnus "Nonnus"). At the start of the epic confrontation between Zeus and [Typhon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typhon "Typhon"), Nonnus has [Nike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nike_\(mythology\) "Nike (mythology)") (Victory) lead Zeus into battle, and Eris lead [Typhon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typhon "Typhon"), and in another passage has Eris, with the war-goddess [Enyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enyo "Enyo"), bring "Tumult" to both sides of a battle.[^97]

## Iconography

There are few certain representations of Eris in art.[^98] Her earliest appearances (mid-sixth-century BC) are found on the [Chest of Cypselus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_of_Cypselus "Chest of Cypselus") and in the tondo of a [black-figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-figure "Black-figure") cup (Berlin F1775).[^99] The geographer [Pausanias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pausanias_\(geographer\) "Pausanias (geographer)") describes seeing Eris depicted on the Chest, as a "most repulsive" \[*aischistê*\] woman standing between [Ajax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajax_the_Great "Ajax the Great") and [Hector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hector "Hector") fighting.[^100] On the cup she is depicted as a normal woman in appearance apart from having wings and winged-sandals.[^101]

From the later part of fifth-century BC, the upper section of a [red-figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red-figure "Red-figure") calyx krater depicts Eris with Themis facing each other, apparently in animated discussion, while the lower section depicts the [Judgement of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judgement_of_Paris "Judgement of Paris"), confirming Eris' role in the events as told in the *[Cypria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cypria "Cypria")*.[^102]

## Cultural influences

The classic [fairy tale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairy_tale "Fairy tale") " [Sleeping Beauty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sleeping_Beauty "Sleeping Beauty") " references what appears to be Eris's role in the wedding of [Peleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peleus "Peleus") and [Thetis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thetis "Thetis"). Like Eris, a malevolent [fairy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairy "Fairy") curses a princess after not being invited to the princess's [christening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infant_baptism "Infant baptism").[^103] [^104]

Eris is the principal figure of worship in the modern [Discordian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discordian "Discordian") religion invented as an "absurdist joke" in 1957 by two school friends [Gregory Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaclypse_the_Younger "Malaclypse the Younger") and [Kerry Wendell Thornley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerry_Wendell_Thornley "Kerry Wendell Thornley"). As mythologized in the religion's satirical text *[Principia Discordia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principia_Discordia "Principia Discordia")*, written by Hill with Thornely and others, Eris (apparently) spoke to Hill and Thornley in an all-night bowling alley, in the form of a chimpanzee.[^105]

The dwarf planet [Eris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eris_\(dwarf_planet\) "Eris (dwarf planet)") was named after this Greek goddess in 2006.[^106]
