### Manuscript Review: Statistical structure and the evolution of languages

---

### 1\. Claim Evaluation

- **Claim 1:** Static word embeddings of human languages exhibit frequency assortativity, where high-popularity words cluster together in semantic space.
	- **Assessment:** Supported. The use of Moran’s I and Geary’s C across 22 languages and four embedding frameworks clearly establishes this spatial correlation.
- **Claim 2:** Semantic spaces demonstrate consistent clustering velocity profiles due to hierarchical aggregation.
	- **Assessment:** Supported. The evidence using both graph-partitioning (modularity) and topological (Betti-0) algorithms yields consistent, non-uniform scaling behaviors.
- **Claim 3:** New words demonstrate persistent temporal dynamics, appearing disproportionately near other recently created entities.
	- **Assessment:** Partly supported. The mathematical evidence is sound, but the dataset for word origination timing is entirely restricted to the English language. Extending this claim to "the evolution of languages" generally is not fully supported by single-language historical data.
- **Claim 4:** The variance in new entity counts scales as a power of the mean over time and space, satisfying Taylor’s law.
	- **Assessment:** Partly supported. The temporal Taylor's law is robust, showing $R^2$ values of 0.69 to 0.88 and exponents consistent with established literature. However, the spatial Taylor's law shows weaker discriminatory power; the authors note that the empirical exponents are close to structureless random processes and $R^2$ levels are not discriminating.
- **Claim 5:** A generative model based on directed preferential placement (DPP) accounts for these empirical regularities better than simpler cumulative-advantage or uniform random models.
	- **Assessment:** Supported. The synthetic data generated by the DPP model successfully mimics the empirical benchmarks across the evaluated metrics, whereas the null models fail.

---

### 2\. Methodological and Statistical Rigor

The methodology is generally sound and utilizes an appropriate blend of natural language processing, spatial geography, and network science. Applying spatial autocorrelation statistics like Moran's I and Geary's C to high-dimensional embedding spaces is an unusual but highly effective and defensible choice for quantifying semantic clustering.

However, there are notable methodological limitations regarding the temporal analyses:

- **Confounding Variables (Polysemy):** The study relies exclusively on static word embeddings, which assign a single vector to each word regardless of its distinct senses. Because the creation of new senses for existing words is abstracted away, the temporal dynamics of "new entity" generation may be heavily confounded by semantic shifts in old words.
- **Arbitrary Spatial Discretization:** The temporal dynamics and Taylor's law evaluations depend on partitioning the $d$ -dimensional unit hypersphere into $K=100$ equal-volumed hypercone segments. The statistical rigor of the clustering dynamics and scaling laws is difficult to verify without knowing how sensitive the regressions are to the choice of $K$.
- **Generalizability:** The title and framing imply universal language properties, but all historical and temporal modeling (claims 3 and 4) relies exclusively on English datasets (Stanford histWord and Merriam-Webster).

---

### 3\. Nuance and Credit

The authors get several things genuinely right. First, the cross-validation strategy for the static spatial claims is excellent. By testing the hypotheses against 22 distinct languages and four different embedding architectures (GloVe, Word2vec, FastText, Polyglot), the authors effectively eliminate the risk that their findings are mere artifacts of specific training algorithms or English-centric biases.

Additionally, the incremental progression of the generative models is logically sound. By starting with a quasi-uniform null model and systematically adding complexity (preferential placement, recency, and finally directed spatial context), the authors isolate exactly which mechanisms are mathematically required to produce the observed empirical structures.

---

### 4\. Recommendations

To improve the validity and clarity of the manuscript, I recommend the following revisions:

1. **Constrain the Scope of Temporal Claims:** Explicitly state in the abstract and conclusion that the temporal dynamics, activity persistence, and Taylor's law findings currently apply only to the English language. Alternatively, introduce historical onset data for at least one non-English language to validate the cross-linguistic claims.
2. **Include a Sensitivity Analysis for Spatial Partitioning:** Provide a supplementary analysis demonstrating the robustness of the temporal dynamics and Taylor's law regressions when the spatial segmentation parameter ($K$) is modified (e.g., $K=50$ and $K=200$). This will rule out the possibility that the observed scaling laws are an artifact of the specific grid size.
3. **Address the Polysemy Confound:** Expand the discussion on static embeddings to formally acknowledge how the inability to track new word senses (polysemy) might skew the temporal clustering metrics. Acknowledge this as a primary limitation of the study's definition of "new word creation."

Here are the primary takeaways for the field:

### 1\. Novel Intrinsic Evaluation Metrics for Latent Spaces

Currently, LLM embeddings are largely evaluated on downstream tasks (e.g., question answering, semantic similarity benchmarks). The authors provide a new set of mathematical benchmarks for intrinsic evaluation.

- **Structural Fidelity:** Engineers could measure a model's latent space using Moran’s I or Geary’s C to check for frequency assortativity. If a model fails to exhibit these clustering velocity profiles or Taylor's law scaling, it may indicate that its representation of human knowledge is mathematically distorted or unnaturally uniform.
- **Diagnosing Bias or Drift:** Comparing an LLM's structural metrics against historical baselines could serve as an early warning system for semantic collapse or catastrophic forgetting during fine-tuning.

### 2\. Targeted Continuous Pre-Training

LLMs struggle to stay up-to-date with current events without undergoing expensive retraining. The authors note that language evolves in "punctuational bursts" within specific, mathematically predictable "hot spots" in the embedding space.

- **Efficient Updating:** Instead of updating the entire network uniformly, model developers could identify which semantic "cones" are currently active (hot spots) and disproportionately direct continuous pre-training resources, data scraping, and compute toward those specific conceptual regions.
- **Preserving Cold Spots:** Conversely, stable "cold spots" could be mathematically frozen or heavily regularized to prevent catastrophic forgetting of established knowledge.

### 3\. Predictive Tokenization and Vocabulary Expansion

The authors’ Directed Preferential Placement (DPP) model suggests that new words are pushed away from dense areas to "seed" new semantic regions, rather than being placed randomly.

- **Anticipating Neologisms:** LLM tokenizers (like Byte-Pair Encoding) are entirely reactive; they only add tokens after they appear frequently in a corpus. If the DPP model accurately maps how new concepts fill spatial voids, developers could use it to theoretically predict where new vocabulary will be needed, dynamically expanding the model's tokenizer or pre-allocating latent vectors for emerging concepts in technology or culture.

### 4\. Better Synthetic Data Generation

Generative AI relies increasingly on synthetic data to train subsequent models. A major flaw in current synthetic data is that it often lacks the statistical richness and long-tail distributions of human data.

- **Simulating Cultural Evolution:** If developers want to simulate future language states, or generate deeply realistic artificial datasets, they can implement the DPP generating process. This would ensure the synthetic data mathematically mirrors the "rich get richer" accumulation and realistic temporal clustering found in true human cultural development.

### 5\. Transitioning from Static to Contextual Dynamics

The authors acknowledge a limitation: they used static embeddings (where a word has one vector) rather than contextual embeddings (like those in LLMs, which handle polysemy).

- **Mapping Sense Divergence:** The immediate takeaway for LLM researchers is to adapt this framework to contextual spaces. If the rules hold, LLMs could be used to mathematically track when a single cluster (a word) begins to split into a new, distinct sub-cluster, providing real-time tracking of polysemy and semantic shifts in human discourse.
