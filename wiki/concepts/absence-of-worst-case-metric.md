# Absence-of-Worst-Case Metric

A failure mode in evaluating high-stakes systems: measuring the system as successful because the *specific worst-case event* hasn't yet occurred, while ignoring the cumulative ongoing cost — financial, moral, ecological, human — that the system imposes in steady state.

## The pattern

When a system credibly threatens catastrophic harm (nuclear exchange, runaway AI, ecological collapse), it becomes natural to evaluate it against the binary question: *did the catastrophe happen?* If no, the system is judged successful and the steady-state cost is treated as the price of stability.

This brackets out everything actually happening:

- the resources consumed maintaining the system
- the populations exposed to its routine operation
- the doctrinal normalizations required to operate it
- the political pathologies that keep it running
- the near-misses that didn't escalate by luck rather than by design

By the absence-of-worst-case metric, any system that hasn't yet destroyed everything is succeeding.

## Nuclear deterrence as the canonical case

The standard claim is that nuclear deterrence "worked" because no nuclear war has occurred since 1945. Holding the metric still and pricing in the substrate:

- ~$10T US expenditure (a comparable Soviet figure helped collapse that economy)
- downwinder populations and test-site cancers spanning generations
- a documented dozen-plus near-misses (Petrov 1983, Able Archer 1983, Norwegian rocket 1995, Cuba 1962, multiple broken-arrow incidents) where escalation was prevented by individual judgment rather than systemic design
- doctrinal embedding of willingness to commit civilian megadeath as a planning baseline
- proliferation that never actually stopped (nine nuclear states and counting)
- treaty regime now visibly decomposing (INF gone, New START expiring, all three major arsenals modernizing)
- moral substrate of the operating populations corroded by eight decades of normalizing the unthinkable as procedure (Anders, Lifton, and the post-Hiroshima literature on this)

The metric reports success. The substrate reports a civilization that paid an enormous price for the *deferral* of the worst case while permanently deforming the moral frame that decides what counts as acceptable harm. The "no nuclear war" win was a deferred bill, not a paid one.

## Why the metric persists

Because the alternative — pricing in steady-state harm — produces unacceptable conclusions for the institutions that benefit from the system. If nuclear deterrence is judged honestly, you get a regime that should have been dismantled. If AI safety is judged honestly *now*, you get a regime that should not be deployed at the scale it is being deployed. The metric is load-bearing precisely because the honest accounting would require action no one with capture over the system will take. See [[institutional-capture-vs-species-framing]].

## Generalization to AI

The same metric is being installed for AI: as long as no superintelligent extinction event has occurred, the system is successful. Civilian casualties from automated targeting (see [[spin-vs-substrate]]), labor displacement, epistemic distortion, concentration of cognitive infrastructure, the moral cost of normalizing autonomous lethal decision-support — these are bracketed as transition costs rather than steady-state harm. The metric is doing the same work, defending the same kind of regime, in the same way it did for nuclear weapons.

## Diagnostic question

For any claim that a high-stakes system is "working": *what is the steady-state cost being bracketed out of this evaluation, and who pays it?* If the answer is "populations and futures with no voice in the metric," the metric is rhetorical, not analytical.
