# Design Thinking

A structured, human-centered framework for solving complex problems through iterative understanding, creative generation, and empirical testing.

## Core Principle

Organize process around the **end-user experience**, not the technology or the solution. Every design decision ultimately serves people. Context determines what counts as good or bad design — there is no context-free quality.

## The 10 Steps (MIT / Kotelly)

The framework is often presented as two phases with very different leverage ratios:

### Front-End (Steps 1–6) — *Where innovation is actually enabled*
Most practitioners underinvest here. Visible output is minimal (research notes, specs), but this is where the **"Experience Centerline"** is established — a shared understanding of the core human need.

| Step | Focus |
|------|-------|
| 1. Identify Needs | Problem in full societal + contextual frame |
| 2. Gather Information | Observe users; find cross-domain analogs |
| 3. Stakeholder Analysis | Full network, not just "users"; seek win-wins |
| 4. Operational Research | Constraints, trade-offs, workarounds |
| 5. Hazard Analysis | Risks as innovation opportunities |
| 6. Specification Creation | Inspiring specs: not too detailed, not too vague |

### Back-End (Steps 7–10) — *Where work becomes visible*
Where most teams spend the majority of time. Without strong front-end work, this phase iterates around the wrong problem.

| Step | Focus |
|------|-------|
| 7. Creative Design | Deep problem immersion; brainstorming; breakthrough stimulus |
| 8. Conceptual Design | Winnow, re-combine, synthesize toward testable directions |
| 9. Prototype Design | Testable models at varying fidelity |
| 10. Verification | Usability testing → refine → re-prototype → stakeholder buy-in |
