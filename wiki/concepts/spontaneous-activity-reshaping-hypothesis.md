# Spontaneous Activity Reshaping Hypothesis

The **Spontaneous Activity Reshaping Hypothesis** (SARH) is a neurological theory developed by [[roger-koenig-robert|Roger Koenig-Robert]] and colleagues. It challenges the traditional "seeing in reverse" model of imagination.

## Core Tenets

1. **Internal Rumbling:** The early visual cortex is constantly active even without external input, producing shifting patterns that resemble real vision ("noise" or "static").
2. **Sculpting by Suppression:** Imagination does not build a signal from scratch. Instead, it uses top-down feedback to selectively *suppress* neurons that are drifting away from the desired image.
3. **Settling Out of Noise:** By holding still the currents of spontaneous activity, the mental image "settles out" like a signal carving its way through static.

## Traditional vs. SARH

| Feature | Traditional Model | SARH Model |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Mechanism** | Feedback *drives* neurons to fire. | Feedback *suppresses*/modulates existing activity. |
| **Process Direction** | Top-down construction. | Sculpting from background noise. |
| **Mental Quality** | Weaker version of sight. | Qualitatively different; carved from internal states. |

## Explanatory Power

- **Distinction:** Explains why we rarely confuse imagination with perception; perception arrives with a strength/regularity that internal sculpting cannot match.
- **[[aphantasia]]:** Suggests that high excitability (over-active noise) makes the system harder to "shape," leading to an inability to form mental images.
