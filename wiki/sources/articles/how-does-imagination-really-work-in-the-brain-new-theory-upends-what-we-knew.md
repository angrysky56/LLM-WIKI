# How does imagination really work in the brain? New theory upends what we knew

Published in *The Conversation* (2026) by [[roger-koenig-robert|Roger Koenig-Robert]], this article outlines a new framework for understanding visual imagination: the [[spontaneous-activity-reshaping-hypothesis]].

## Key Insights

- **Background Activity:** The brain uses ~99% of its energy on internal, spontaneous activity (noise/static) rather than processing external inputs or tasks.
- **Imagination as Carving:** Instead of visual imagination being "seeing in reverse" (building a signal from top-down feedback), the theory suggests the brain *sculpts* images by suppressing unwanted currents in the early visual workshop.
- **Feedback as Modulation:** Feedback signals don't drive neurons to fire like light does; they increase or decrease existing activity.
- **Signal through Static:** Mental images are signals carved out of existing internal "rumblings" of neural activity.
- **Clinical Connections:**
    - [[aphantasia]]: High excitability in early visual areas makes the spontaneous patterns harder to "hold in shape" or carve.
    - **Hyperphantasia:** The opposite extreme, where carving is exceptionally precise.
