# The 10 Steps to Design Thinking (MIT)

**Source:** https://professional.mit.edu/news/articles/10-steps-design-thinking  
**Author:** Blade Kotelly, MIT Senior Lecturer  
**Published:** 2019-02-15

## Core Insight

Design thinking is a structured framework for human-centered innovation. The critical mistake most practitioners make is spending the majority of time on steps 7–10 (design/testing) when the real leverage is in steps 1–6 (research/framing). Kotelly calls the output of those early steps the **"Experience Centerline"** — a deep collective understanding of the core human need being served.

## The 10 Steps

| # | Step | Key Point |
|---|------|-----------|
| 1 | **Identify Needs** | Most critical & difficult; understand problem in full societal context |
| 2 | **Gather Information** | Observe users in their environment; find analogs from other domains |
| 3 | **Stakeholder Analysis** | Beyond users — map the full network; seek win-win with non-traditional stakeholders |
| 4 | **Operational Research** | Identify constraints (money, time, resources); find workarounds or trade-off balances |
| 5 | **Hazard Analysis** | Risks can be innovation opportunities (e.g., overdraft protection) |
| 6 | **Specification Creation** | Specs in the middle: not too detailed (limits innovation), not too vague (unanchored) |
| 7 | **Creative Design** | Brainstorming + deep problem immersion; change brain chemistry for breakthrough thinking |
| 8 | **Conceptual Design** | Winnow and re-combine; synthesize research with ideas to select directions for testing |
| 9 | **Prototype Design** | Build testable models at varying fidelity (paper to functional) |
| 10 | **Verification** | Usability testing → learn → refine → re-prototype; synthesize data for stakeholder buy-in |
