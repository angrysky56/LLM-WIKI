# Obsidian PARA — byarbrough Template

**Source:** https://github.com/byarbrough/obsidian-para  
**Original:** Tiago Forte's PARA method via Forte Labs

## What is PARA?

**P**rojects — **A**reas — **R**esources — **A**rchives. A universal organization system designed for actionability. The defining constraints:

- **4 categories wide** (Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives)
- **4 levels deep maximum** (Vault → Folders → Notes — no deeper nesting)
- **One sub-folder per PARA category** — never hunt through nested hierarchies

## The Four Categories

| Category | Definition | Actionability |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Projects** | Series of tasks linked to a goal, with a deadline | Active, immediate |
| **Areas** | Sphere of activity with a standard maintained over time | Ongoing, no deadline |
| **Resources** | Topic or theme of ongoing interest | Reference, passive |
| **Archives** | Inactive items from the other three | Dormant |

## Progressive Summarization

Every time you interact with a note, compress it slightly more. Trade some original context for better connectivity with other ideas. The goal: notes that are **discoverable for your future self**.

## Migration Strategy

1. Move all existing content to `4. Archives/<today's date>` (nothing deleted)
2. Create project folders in `1. Projects` with explicit goals
3. Add `2. Areas` folders as needed (don't over-create)
4. Leave `3. Resources` empty — populate only as you encounter material
5. Pull items from Archive only when you need them → move to correct location

## Key Rules

- If a note fits two folders → put it where you'll **most likely need it next**
- Same concept can exist in both Areas and Resources (e.g., `Areas/Health` = your health; `Resources/Health` = general notes)
- Maintain the hierarchy across **all** your systems (files, cloud, notes) for maximum findability
